Why local-first: privacy and latency vs the cloud

Cloud dictation sends your voice to a server and back; Sussurro keeps every step on your machine — here's the trade that makes, honestly.

5 min read · Concept

Most voice dictation tools work the same way: your microphone audio streams to a server, gets transcribed there, and the result streams back. That round-trip is invisible when it works, but it means every word you say leaves your machine first. Sussurro skips that step entirely — capture, transcription, and AI cleanup all happen on the device you're sitting at.

This isn't a minor implementation detail. It's the decision that shapes everything else about the app.

What runs where

The full pipeline — global hotkey, microphone capture, speech-to-text with whisper.cpp or NVIDIA Parakeet, LLM cleanup through a local Ollama model, then paste-injection into whatever app has focus — never opens a network socket to send your voice or your text anywhere. There's no account to create, because there's no server-side identity to attach one to. There's no telemetry, because there's no backend collecting it.

CONCRETELY

No login screen, no API key, nothing to "sign in" to before you can dictate. The diagnostics button in the footer copies version, OS, and configuration for a bug report — never your dictated text or dictionary.

One practical consequence: Sussurro works with no internet connection at all. On a plane, on a train through a tunnel, on a network that's just having a bad day — dictation, cleanup, and paste-injection all keep working, because none of them were waiting on a response from somewhere else. See how the pipeline works for the full request path.

The honest trade-off

Local-first isn't free. A cloud service can put a much larger model behind your request than most laptops can run comfortably, and it can update that model overnight without you doing anything. Running Whisper or Parakeet locally means the model size you pick is bounded by your hardware, and updating it is a deliberate action — download a new one in Settings — not something that happens to you.

The cleanup step has the same shape. Sussurro's AI cleanup runs against a small instruct model through Ollama, not a frontier model with a huge context window. That's a real ceiling on how ambitious the cleanup instructions can be. It's also why Sussurro is built to degrade honestly rather than pretend the ceiling isn't there: if cleanup would time out or Ollama isn't running, the app falls back to the raw transcript instead of hanging or failing silently.

WHAT YOU'RE TRADING

Cloud dictation buys you a bigger model and zero setup. Sussurro buys you audio that never leaves the device, no account, no subscription, offline operation, and the ability to swap the underlying model yourself — see choosing a cleanup model. Neither is strictly better; they're different bets.

Why this is the default, not an option

A lot of "privacy-focused" software really means "privacy-focused, with a cloud fallback for the features that matter." Sussurro doesn't have that fallback to fall back to — there's no server component at all. The local scripting API is loopback-only by default for the same reason: even a feature meant for your own scripts doesn't listen on the network unless you turn it on.

The result is closer to owning a tool than renting one. Your dictation history lives in a local JSONL file you can inspect, export, or delete (see where data lives); the model you dictate against is one you chose and can replace. Latency is also just physics working in your favor — there's no request queue, no server load, no "cloud is busy" — the only delay is however fast the model runs on the hardware in front of you.
